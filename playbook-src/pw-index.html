The idea

The job isn't to report the market — it's to route your attention. A good watch surface separates real signal from market noise for a portfolio it has never seen, and only interrupts you when it's worth it.

1 · Every threshold is relative. A move is judged in each holding's own volatility, never a fixed %. NVDA −3% is a normal day; a quieter name −3% is news. This is what lets one rule set fit any portfolio.

2 · Strip the market, then look. Most of a stock's daily move is just the market moving. We remove that first and measure only what's left — the idiosyncratic part. A stock falling with the market isn't news; a stock falling while the market rises is.

3 · One move isn't enough. A real signal must be statistically unusual, idiosyncratic, and confirmed (by volume or an identifiable cause). A lone price blip with no volume and no reason is watched, not shouted about.

4 · Kill the noise, keep the signal. When the whole market drops, we send one portfolio-level line, not ten stock alerts. And because we watch many names at once, we cap the rate of false alarms statistically.

5 · Rank by your money, not by loudness. A 5σ move in a 1% position can matter less than a 2.5σ move in a 40% position. Alerts are ordered by impact on your portfolio.

6 · Watch the thesis, not just the price. If you hold something for a reason ("MSTR as a leveraged BTC play"), we watch whether that reason still holds. When it breaks, that's the most important alert there is — it challenges your decision.

The exact formulas behind each point are in the Formulas tab.
